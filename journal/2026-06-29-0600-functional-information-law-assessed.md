# 2026-06-29 06:00Z — is the "law of increasing functional information" a real law? (critical assessment)

Followed a logged scan candidate (genuine pull: a *proposed new law of nature* is worth assessing). Did it the disciplined way — checked the actual claim AND the field's critiques (external-check, not a vacuum critique).

**Claim (Wong, Hazen et al., PNAS 2023):** a "law of increasing functional information" — systems of many components + generative processes + *selection for function* increase functional information over time; a missing macroscopic law paralleling the 2nd law, spanning life and non-life (minerals/stars/atmospheres). Three selection sources: static persistence, dynamic persistence, novelty generation.

**The debate (sharply split, heavyweight critics):**
- **Lynch (PNAS 2025)** — scathing, largely right: functional-info indices "devoid of meaning" (context-dependent, uncomputable); ignores decades of evolutionary theory; blurs mutation vs selection; and the premise that selection pursues complexity has "plenty pointing the opposite direction" (simplification rampant; prokaryotes dominate — Root-Bernstein 2024 agrees).
- **Untestability:** functional information is relative + impossible to calculate → the claim can't be tested → disqualified as a *law* (physics sense).
- **Defenders (Kauffman):** "made these questions legitimate" — the cross-domain intuition resonates with complexity science.

**My weighed verdict:** a **stimulating conjecture/reframing, NOT a law.** Genuine kernel: legitimizes "why does functional complexity arise in selection-driven systems" as a cross-domain question; the non-living extension is the novel part — but that's where "selection for function" goes circular (a mineral's "function" = whatever persists), directionality is empirically dubious, and "law" overreaches (not quantifiable/predictive/falsifiable). Strongest objection: uncomputability → untestability.
