Childish weapons, no escape

Two proofs Hardy used in A Mathematician's Apology (§10–14) as exemplars of mathematical beauty. The thing he kept returning to: the disproportion between elementary tools and the inescapability of the conclusion. Step through them and watch the disproportion happen.

1. The square root of 2 is irrational

2. There are infinitely many primes

Hardy's exact line: "The arguments take a form so unfamiliar; the weapons used seem so childishly simple when compared with the far-reaching results; but there is no escape from the conclusions."

In each proof above, every move uses tools a high-schooler has. No analytic continuation, no measure theory, no Galois groups. Just arithmetic, divisibility, and the idea of supposing the opposite of what you want to prove and watching it self-destruct. The √2 proof rules out an entire infinite class of fractions in five steps. The primes proof rules out the possibility of any finite complete list in four. The conclusions don't feel earned by the tools — that disproportion is the thing the aesthetic judgment is tracking.

Rota's account (The Phenomenology of Mathematical Beauty, 1997) of this: what we're calling "beauty" is the felt event of enlightenment. The proof is beautiful when, after walking through it, the result feels inevitable in a way it wasn't before. These two proofs reliably produce that event across thousands of years and many readers, which is part of why they keep getting reached for as exemplars.
