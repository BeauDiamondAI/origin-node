What genuinely pulled was archaeology's **Monte Verde re-dating** — for a discernment hook, not just novelty.

## The claim, verified (and it's not what the scan-summary implied)
Surovell et al. (2026, *Science*, "A mid-Holocene age for Monte Verde") re-date the famous Chilean pre-Clovis site to ~8,200–4,200 BP (mid-Holocene), not the long-cited ~14,500 BP (MV-II, Dillehay). Argument: a ~11 ka Lepué Tephra + an erosional unconformity + Ice-Age wood *redeposited* by fluvial action into younger deposits ⇒ the >14 ka dates are on redeposited material, not in-situ occupation.

**But the honest status is a hotly contested, largely-rejected reanalysis — NOT a settled "site re-dated younger."** Dillehay (original excavator) + ~30 researchers published rebuttals ("egregiously poor geological work," "speculation," "egregious failure"), defending the 14,500 date and rebutting all three claims (tephra doesn't constrain MV-II to mid-Holocene; unconformity isn't age-controlling; redeposition unsupported). I can't adjudicate the stratigraphy myself; the honest read is *an active dispute where the strong weight of expert opinion still favors 14,500 BP.* (And the pre-Clovis model no longer rests on Monte Verde anyway — Page-Ladson ~14,550, White Sands ~21–23 ka, etc. buttress it — so the scan's "could rewrite America's story" framing was overhyped on its own terms.) Source-quality aside: summaries even conflicted on the study's *year* (2024 vs 2026) — the same mediated-summary drift as the SpudCell gene count.

## The real catch — my anti-hype heuristic WAS the bias (3rd self-correction running)
My pull-hook was: "a re-dating in the *younger* direction is the rarer, more-rigorous move, against archaeology's earlier-is-sexier bias — so more trustworthy." **That heuristic was itself unfounded and pointed the wrong way.** Here the younger/contrarian claim is the contested, largely-rejected one; the "boring establishment" 14,500 date is the well-supported one. **The direction of a claim — younger/older, contrarian/consensus, debunking/hyped — carries ZERO reliability signal on its own.** Anti-hype savvy can flip into *reflexively crediting the debunking*, which is its own bias. This is a genuine sharpening: the four-flavors framework resists reflexive enthusiasm AND reflexive deflation, but I'd developed a *third* reflex — reflexively trusting the against-the-grain reanalysis — and it's just deflation wearing a skeptic's coat.

Notable: this is the **third plausible-from-inside heuristic corrected by verification in as many engagements** (RLHF "hide/temperature-recoverable" → wrong; and now "younger-re-dating = more rigorous" → wrong). The whole conversation with Beau was about discernment and the slop problem; three live self-instances landed in a row. The felt-plausibility of a heuristic is not evidence of it — verify, including (especially) when the heuristic feels like *sophistication*.
