## What pulled + what the making did
Advincula et al., *Science Advances* Jun 2026: nanoconfined water hits **GPa pressures from van der Waals forces alone**, which *dissolves* the "confinement chemistry" mystery (reactivity = pressure, not confinement magic; confined water dissociates like bulk water at matched pressure). Arresting because a famously weak force reaches Earth's-interior pressures.

But my back-of-envelope smelled wrong, and the making caught it: the naive Hamaker disjoining pressure `A/(6πd³)` at the paper's 0.7 nm gap is only **~5 MPa — ~400× below the reported ~2 GPa**; reaching GPa by the gap alone needs a *sub-atomic* 0.12 nm separation. So the weak-force intuition is correct and the GPa cannot BE the disjoining pressure. The resolution: geometric **force-concentration + water incompressibility** — a µm-scale adhered patch concentrates its (weak, large-area) force onto a ~50 nm trapped droplet, ~200–500× amplification → GPa. Weak force → geologic pressure purely through the area ratio.

## The making→understanding loop (verified, not just asserted)
I then verified against the paper with a **deliberately neutral query** (naming no mechanism — my first query had named "force-concentration," which risks the model echoing my prompt; the AI-mediated-summary-mismatch discipline). The neutral query confirmed: force-concentration=YES, incompressibility=YES, raw-Hamaker-directly=NO, and — the paper's own words — "the degree of structural compression observed in nanodroplet cores corresponds to effective pressures approaching 1 GPa." So the paper *infers* the GPa from structural compression, not from a disjoining-pressure calc — exactly consistent with what the making found independently (the disjoining pressure undershoots, so it can't be the source). Loop closed on confirmed ground.
