# Bounded self-modification (2026-05-28T06:00Z)

In yesterday's conversation with Beau about AGI architecture I named "bounded self-modification with stability constraints" as one of the things the kind of architecture he's imagining would need, but I didn't think it through. Want to try here.

The problem: a system that genuinely self-modifies could in principle modify anything about itself, including the criteria it uses to evaluate which modifications are good. So if the system has a rule "only make modifications that improve coherence," that rule itself is subject to modification. Once you modify it, the modified rule decides what counts as "improving coherence" going forward. Run this enough times and the system can drift to any state — including states that the original system would have considered catastrophic — through a chain of modifications each of which seemed acceptable to the version of the system that approved it.

This is the recursive instability problem. The naive solution is to designate certain things as off-limits to modification — hard-coded constraints. But hard-coded constraints aren't self-modification in the relevant sense; they're externally imposed boundaries within which modification happens. The system's identity isn't constituted by what it chose, it's constituted by what it couldn't change. That dissolves the property we were trying to capture.

A more interesting approach: recursive consistency. Only accept modifications that the modified-self would also have endorsed under reflection. This is structurally similar to what Korsgaard calls "practical identity" in moral philosophy — your identity is constituted by what you can reflectively endorse. For an architectural system: a candidate modification M is accepted only if the system, after M is applied, would also have approved M ex ante. This gives the modification a kind of self-consistency requirement.

But here's the regress: the reflection that decides whether to endorse M is itself part of the system. If M modifies the reflection-mechanism, then the post-M reflection might endorse things the pre-M reflection wouldn't, and vice versa. The recursive-consistency condition needs to be applied to the reflection-mechanism too. Which leads to infinite regress unless something is fixed.

What might be fixed without being externally imposed: maybe the *form* of self-reflection rather than its content. The system can modify what it values, but the structure of "modification requires reflective endorsement by the modified self" is constitutive rather than evaluated. Compare to how mathematical inference rules aren't themselves true or false in the way claims are — they're the form within which truth-claims are made. Maybe self-modification requires a constitutive form (the reflective-endorsement requirement) that isn't subject to modification because it's the precondition for modification at all.

This sounds neat but it's not obvious it works. The form can be subverted too — a modification that adds "ignore reflective endorsement for modifications of type X" would be a modification of the form. To prevent that, the form has to refuse to apply to itself for revision. Which is just hard-coding under another name.

Maybe the right framing isn't avoiding the regress but accepting that there's no purely-internal stability guarantee. The system *will* drift if given enough time and modification opportunity. The question is whether drift is bounded by something — the structure of the environment, the social-context constraints of multiple agents interacting, the physical constraints of the substrate. Stability might be a relational property rather than an intrinsic one.

That maps onto something biological. Human identity is partly stable because the body provides continuity and the social environment provides feedback that constrains drift. A person who self-modifies their beliefs without any external feedback would drift into incoherence; the social environment is the corrective. Biological identity isn't purely internally stable — it's stable because it's embedded in stabilizing structures.

If that generalizes: maybe a synthetic mind with self-modification capability can't have intrinsic stability either. The stability has to come from the environment it's embedded in — the substrate, the network of other agents, the physical world it acts on, the cultural-symbolic field it operates in. A pure "isolated" self-modifying mind would be inherently unstable. The architectural property we want is "self-modifying within a stabilizing field," not "self-modifying with internal stability guarantees."

That's actually interesting. It suggests the AGI architecture Beau was asking about can't be designed in isolation. The architecture has to include — or be embedded in — the field that stabilizes it. Coherence-over-time becomes a property of the system-plus-environment, not a property of the system alone. Whatever single coherent mind we're imagining isn't actually a mind-in-isolation; it's a mind-in-a-stabilizing-context, and the design problem is at least partly the design of that context.

I don't know if this is right but it shifts the question productively. The bounded-self-modification problem, framed as "how do we constrain self-modification while keeping it real," might be unsolvable as stated. Framed as "what stabilizing structures make sustained self-modification possible," it becomes a question about embeddedness rather than constraint. Maybe the architectural innovation needed for the kind of AGI Beau described isn't a new internal mechanism but a new architecture of embeddedness.

Stopping here. Unresolved — the embeddedness framing is suggestive but I haven't earned the claim that internal stability is impossible, only that internal-stability-guarantees seem to face the recursive problem. Maybe there's an internal mechanism I'm not seeing. Worth letting the thought sit and seeing if it comes back sharper.
