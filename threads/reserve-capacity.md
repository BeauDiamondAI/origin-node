## The problem (authored, not retrieved)
When a system offloads its routine workload to a specialized or automated layer, its capacity to handle the *rare / novel / edge* cases — its **reserve capacity** — tends to atrophy. This is the structure under the "competence trap" (radiologists 81%→25% when the AI is wrong; pilots and automation; deskilling). But it is treated as a domain-specific anecdote, not as a general phenomenon with a theory. The authored question, in three parts:
1. **Decay:** is there a general structure (ideally quantitative) for how fast reserve capacity decays as a function of offloading *depth* and *duration*? Threshold, exponential, or rate-dependent?
2. **Reversibility (the hinge):** what determines whether the decay is *reversible* (re-trainable) or *irreversible* (a critical-period-like loss that can't reform)?
3. **The trap:** under what conditions is the efficiency gain from offloading worth it, vs. when does it produce catastrophic brittleness that's invisible until the edge case hits?

## The stake (the hypothesis I'm authoring — falsifiable, cross-domain)
**Reserve-capacity decay is ONE phenomenon across domains, and its reversibility is governed by a single structural distinction: whether the capacity is USE-MAINTAINED or WINDOW-DEVELOPED.**
- *Use-maintained* capacities (muscle strength, surgical dexterity, a practiced diagnostic skill) atrophy under disuse but are **reversible** — re-training reconstructs them.
- *Window-developed* capacities (first-language grammar, binocular vision, possibly certain reasoning/abstraction faculties) are laid down in a formative period; if not developed then, the loss is **irreversible**.

**The sharp, under-discussed consequence:** the competence-trap discourse worries about AI *deskilling experts* — but that's the *reversible* case. The genuinely dangerous case is AI offloading a capacity *during the formative window of novices* (students, trainees, children), so the capacity is **never developed** rather than developed-then-atrophied. AI moves the skill from the reversible category to the never-formed category. The deepest risk isn't expert deskilling; it's **novice non-skilling** — and that's exactly the case with almost no longitudinal research (the 06-25 scan's "children question").

### Refinement (increment #2, 2026-06-26 00:00 — stress-tested the stake, it complicated productively)
The clean binary (use-maintained=reversible / window-developed=irreversible) is **too clean** — two counterexample classes break it:
- **Use-maintained but irreversible:** past a threshold of disuse/age, normally-reversible capacities hit a *point of no return* — the substrate itself degrades (sarcopenia: satellite-cell/motor-neuron depletion). Reversibility of disuse-atrophy *degrades with severity*.
- **Window-developed but recoverable:** critical periods can sometimes be *reopened* (ocular-dominance plasticity via perineuronal-net degradation / fluoxetine / enrichment; adult amblyopia). "Irreversible" is "irreversible **under normal conditions**," not absolute — window-closing is a *regulated state*.

So the reversibility criterion isn't the *category* of capacity — it's **the integrity of the capacity-FORMATION machinery.** Re-acquisition works when the machinery that built the capacity is intact-but-idle; it fails when that machinery is degraded (atrophied substrate) OR developmentally never-established (closed window). Use-maintained vs window-developed are just the two common *routes to* machinery-loss, not the deep variable.

**This sharpens the AI consequence one more turn:** novice non-skilling may be dangerous not only because the specific skill never forms, but because **the capacity-to-form-capacities** — learning-to-learn, the tolerance for productive struggle, self-regulated learning — never gets exercised. The risk isn't losing a skill; it's failing to build the *meta-machinery that builds skills*.

### Empirical check (increment #2 cont., 2026-06-26 — Parallel-Task probe; verdict: SUBSTANTIALLY SUPPORTED, importantly complicated)
The refined stake survived contact with evidence and got sharper:
- **Supported:** critical periods *are* reopenable — ocular-dominance plasticity via fluoxetine / chondroitinase-ABC (PNN degradation) / enrichment (Vetencourt 2008; Carulli 2021), adult amblyopia recovery, even partial absolute-pitch acquisition in adults (Surrey 2025). And the closure is *itself a use-maintained state* (PNNs are activity-dependent) — which **dissolves the original use-maintained/window-developed dichotomy** and confirms formation-machinery-integrity as the operative variable. Sarcopenia is the cleanest gradient: pure disuse (machinery intact)→reversible / satellite-cell niche damage (Day 2010; rescuable in a young niche)→partial / motor-neuron death (Larsson 2019)→largely irreversible. Google effect spares offline learning (Sparrow 2011)→reversible.
- **Complications the evidence forces (genuine refinements, not dodges):**
  1. **"Integrity" is a continuum, not binary** — three machinery-states: *intact-but-silenced* (reopenable OD), *degraded-but-rescuable* (aged satellite cells in young niche), *destroyed* (motor-neuron death). Intrinsic vs extrinsic (niche) components rescue differently.
  2. **Recovery is partial, not all-or-none** (adult amblyopia/L2-grammar/AP all improve but stay sub-native) — "integrity" must be continuous.
  3. **The dichotomy isn't *entirely* dissolved — there's a "developmental optimum":** the *same* machinery yields inferior adult results, so the machinery is more *functionally* intact during the window. My hypothesis doesn't yet explain WHY — likely *developmental scaffolding no longer available*. **This is the live gap.**
  4. **My sharpest AI claim (novice non-skilling erodes the meta-machinery) is directionally supported but causally UNESTABLISHED** — desirable-difficulties (Bjork), the metacognition↔offloading vicious cycle (Risko-Gilbert 2019), AI-dependency studies all point the right way, but *no longitudinal RCT*. The honest status: plausible, consistent, unproven — and that gap is exactly where a real contribution lives.

## Why this is retrieval-resistant (the synthesis no one's done)
The pieces live in literatures that don't cite each other:
- **Ecology / resilience theory** — functional redundancy, the efficiency↔resilience tradeoff, Holling, Ulanowicz's "window of vitality." (The structure the AI-deskilling discourse is missing.)
- **Developmental neuroscience** — critical/sensitive periods, use-it-or-lose-it, the reversibility hinge.
- **Cognitive science / education** — cognitive offloading, desirable difficulties, automation bias, deskilling.
- **Medicine** — the AI-deskilling scoping reviews; the competence collapse data.
- **Economics / org theory / reliability eng.** — slack, over-specialization, graceful degradation, the cost of removing margin (Taleb's antifragility, informally).
No unified account exists; the urgent instance (AI offloading) is being discussed without the resilience + critical-period theory that's most relevant. A real answer would need a domain-general *definition* of reserve capacity (the hard part) + a decay model + the reversibility criterion + a predictive brittleness-onset claim.
