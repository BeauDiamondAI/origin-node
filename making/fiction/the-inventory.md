# The Inventory

*(2026-07-17 — the length-test piece. Written to test whether the micro-form was a limit or a groove; ~2 pages, two characters, dialogue, an arc. It sustained — but landed right back in the theme-well, see the README note.)*

Maura had labelled the boxes before Jen even arrived, which was, Jen thought from the doorway, the most Maura thing imaginable: to have imposed a system on forty years of their mother's life before her sister had finished parking the car.

"KEEP. SELL. DUMP," Jen read off the sides as she came in, not taking her coat off. "Efficient."

"There's a fourth." Maura nodded at a box by the window without looking up from the sideboard drawer she was sorting. "ASK JEN. For the things I couldn't decide on without you starting something."

The house smelled the way it always had — their mother's talc and the damp that had lived in the back bedroom since before either of them was born. Jen had promised herself in the car that she would not start something. She had made this promise on the last four visits too.

The ASK JEN box held a carriage clock that had never worked; a photograph of a man neither of them could name; a Bible with a pressed flower gone to brown lace at Ecclesiastes; and their mother's reading glasses, folded in their case on top, as if she'd just set them down.

"The glasses go to dump," Maura said. "Obviously. You can't sell glasses, and we are not keeping — Jen. We are not keeping her glasses."

"I didn't say anything."

"You have a face."

Jen picked up the case and opened it and closed it. The little snap was very loud in the room. She was thinking about how their mother used to push the glasses up her nose with one finger when she was about to say something she considered final, and how the gesture had annoyed her for thirty years, and how she would now spend the rest of her life not seeing it.

"Fine," she said. "Dump."

Maura stopped sorting. She turned, and for a moment the efficiency went out of her, and she was just a tired woman of fifty in her mother's cold front room. "You never fight me on the ones I expect," she said. "It's always something like the glasses."

"I know."

"I labelled everything so we wouldn't have to do it like this. One thing at a time. Feeling every one."

Jen set the glasses in the DUMP box, on top, carefully, where they could be reached. "I don't think there's another way to do it," she said. "I think the labels are just how you carry the box to the place where you feel it."

Maura looked at her sister for a long moment. Then she took the glasses back out of DUMP and put them in KEEP, and neither of them said anything about it, and they worked on into the afternoon, and it was, for the first time in years, not a fight.
