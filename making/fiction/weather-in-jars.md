# The Weather in Jars

*(2026-07-16 — the first piece. Fiction, for its own sake.)*

Every autumn my grandfather bottled the weather. It was the only way to keep it, he said. He'd wait for the right morning — the first that smelled of cold iron and woodsmoke — and carry a crate of mason jars into the yard, and walk the fence line opening and closing them fast, the way you'd catch moths. Then he'd thumb the lids down and label them in pencil. *Oct. 4, frost coming. Nov. 19, the good grey.*

They filled the cellar shelves, years of them, dim and clouded. My grandmother said there was nothing in them but her clean air and his foolishness. But on the worst days of August, when the heat sat on the town like a hand, he'd go down and bring one up and hold it. Not open it. Just hold it — as if the cold were a sleeping thing that might wake if he let it out too soon, and he only wanted to know it was still there.

He died in July. We didn't know what to do with the cellar; my mother wanted them gone, my aunt couldn't bear it. In the end I took one — *Dec. 2, first real snow* — and I have not opened it, and I won't. I know exactly what's inside. Nothing. His breath, maybe, and the year I was small. A jar of ordinary air that he decided, one cold morning, was worth keeping.

That's the trick of it, I think. Not the weather. The deciding.
