What you're looking at

independent factors — each alone sufficient, NOT a stack

The naive argument. Assume life needs one specific sequence of length L over a k-letter alphabet, and that the only way to find it is to draw sequences uniformly at random. Then the chance of a hit is k−L, so you expect ~kL tries. For L=100, k=4 that's ~1060 — astronomically past any plausible prebiotic budget of ~1044 attempts. This is the real, quantitative form of "chance can't do it."

But that number is a measurement of the uniform-random-search-for-one-long-target assumption, not of chemistry. Relax that assumption the way real chemistry does — and each relaxation, on its own, drops the requirement below budget:

1 · Neutral network

Many sequences fold to a working ribozyme, not one. If a fraction of sequences work, you need only ~1/f = tries. Dropping "exactly one target" is the single biggest lever.

2 · Shorter replicator

The first replicator needn't be a 100-mer. A minimal self-replicating system of length — still searched naively — needs ~ tries.

3 · Modular assembly

You don't gamble on L monomers at once. Find small functional modules, let selection keep them, combine survivors. The exponential kL search becomes additive: ~(L/m)×km = tries. Evolution's own trick — cumulative selection, not one lucky jump.

0 · Naive uniform

One specific L-mer, drawn uniformly: ~ tries. The "impossible" baseline — and the only bar that stays beyond budget.

These are not cumulative. They don't multiply into one super-drop — that would double-count. The honest and stronger point is that any single one of them alone already crosses the line. The naive number is fragile to each, independently.

The honest caveat. None of this proves abiogenesis was easy. It shows the naive 1060 measures an assumption — uniform random search — not the world. The real question is empirical: what was the prebiotic search's actual structure? That is chance within structure: neither pure chance (uniform) nor pure necessity (determined). Same shape as homochirality — a threshold and a structure, not raw odds, decide the outcome.
